Overview

The term fractal was first coined by Beno√Æt Mandelbrot who defined a fractal as "a rough or fragmented geometric shape that can be subdivided in parts, each of which is (at least approximately) a reduced-size copy of the whole." In his definition, Mandelbrot described the self-similarity property of fractals, meaning that it is an exact (or approximate) copy of part of itself. Fractals can be made by repeating a simple process over and over again indefinitely. This recurring loop makes a never-ending pattern that is visually appealing.

Fractal Dimension

The fractal dimension of a fractal roughly gives you a sense of how complex the pattern is. This measure is calculated by dividing the logarithm of the number of self-similar pieces the figure has (N) by the logarithm of the recipricol of the dilation factor (r). This formula seems random, but by applying it to simpler figures, we can see the logic.
We know that a line is one dimensional, ie. has a dimension of 1. If we take a line and split it into x self-similar pieces, the dilation factor would be 1/x. These pieces are self-similar simply if they are of equal length. If the original line is of length 1, the self-similar pieces are of length 1/x. Applying the dimension formula, we get:
  dimension = log(N) / log(1/r) = log(x) / log(x) = 1
We know that polygons are two dimensional. If we take a square and split it into 4 self-similar squares, each square has a side length equal to 1/2 the length of the original square. Applying the dimension formula, we get:
  dimension = log(N) / log(1/r) = log(4) / log(2) = log(22) / log(2) = 2*log(2) / log(2) = 2

Sierpinski Triangle

N = 1

The Sierpinski Triangle is an example of a fractal whose self-similar property can be easily seen. The figure itself is an exact copy of the three smaller equilateral triangle, whose side lengths are half that of the whole figure's, at the top, bottom right, and bottom left vertices.

The process of making this fractal can be explained in these three steps:
 1. Take an equilateral triangle.
 2. Shrink the triangle by a factor of 1/2 (r = 1/2) and place three of these triangles in each of the three vertices of the original triangle.
 3. Repeat step 2 with each of the three newly placed triangles.

Oftentimes, there is more than one way to think about how a fractal is made. Another process that creates this same fractal can be described as the following:
 1. Take an equilateral triangle.
 2. Divide the triangle into four smaller congruent triangles. Each smaller triangle should have a side length that is equal to half the original triangle.
 3. Remove the central triangle.
 4. Repeat steps 2 and 3 with each of the remaining three triangles.

Hexagon Fractal

N = 1

This hexagon fractal is also self-similar as it is an exact copy of the 6 smaller hexagons, whose side lengths are a third that of the whole figure's, at each of the vertices.

The process of making this fractal can be explained in these three steps:
 1. Take a regular hexagon.
 2. Shrink the hexagon by a factor of 1/3 (r = 1/3) and place six of these hexagons in each of the six vertices of the original hexagon.
 3. Repeat step 2 with each of the six newly placed hexagons.

Square Fractal

N = 1

This square fractal can also be classified as self-similar. If we ignore the coloring of the squares, the figure is an exact copy of the square rotated 45° that is inscribed in the larger square.

The process of making this fractal can be explained in these three steps:
 1. Take a square.
 2. Shrink the square by a factor of √2 / 2 (r = √2 / 2), rotate it clockwise 45°, and place it in the center of the original square.
 3. Repeat step 2 with the new square.

 1. Take a square and divide it into four purple triangles in each vertex and a white (or empty) rotated square in the center.
